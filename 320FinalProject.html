What affects COVID Vaccination rates the most?¶

By Hafsa Karim and Matthee Sabulao¶

Published May 16th, 2022¶

In December of 2019, the first cluster of Covid cases occurred in Wuhan, China, and since then, a global pandemic caused the world to collectively experience significant changes in both lifestyle and hygiene practices. However, the introduction of multiple vaccines has given hope towards a lifestyle that resembles a pre-Covid time without fear of contraction or constantly wearing masks. Furthermore, in America, a divide within society caused a split between those who support vaccination versus those who are against vaccination. As a result, some vaccines were presumably distributed in locations that did not use them.

The purpose of our project is to attempt to predict where vaccines should be distributed on income and age. This is beneficial because vaccines are still not easily accessible throughout the world, so if we can maximize efficiency then there would be more resources for those who need them. We hypothesize that if income is higher and population consists of a significant amount of person's 65 years and older, then individuals are more likely to be vaccinated. Although many other factors contribute to the vaccination rate, the reason we chose to analyze income is that we believe that higher income means higher education which would mean individuals are more likely to be informed about the benefits of vaccination. In addition, the reason why we chose to analyze the age is that we believe that individuals older than 65 are more likely to be vaccinated since they are the most vulnerable, and would feel more inclined in order to protect their health.

Conclusion¶

Based on our scatterplots above, the distribution of vaccines would be more efficient and less wasteful if we prioritized giving areas with higher median income as they appear to be more likely to use them. However, it is important to note that there may be other reasons as to why the vaccination rate of higher income families is high. For example, these families might have more accesibility to vaccines since higher income means they reside in an area with better living conditions.

In regards to the influence of the percent of persons 65 years and older versus the vaccination rate, the data provided and the linear regression line is not significant enough to show a trend or conclusive results. This might be because the age is not an indicator of vaccination rates as we predicted in our hypothesis. We found similar non-conclusive results when analyzing the influence of the percent of persons under 65 years versus vaccination rate, as the linear regression and scatterplots did not appear sufficient and have a strong linear spread. The similarities between these two scatterplots and regression linears adds reassurance towards the lack of influence that the percent age of the population has on vaccination rate.

Overall, there are many other factors that could influence vaccination rate such as race, behavior, or political affiliation. Based on our results, we could save resources by distributing to high income families regardless of age. If we were to do this project again, we would include more counties and a larger dataset to get more conclusive results.
